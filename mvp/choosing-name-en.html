Choosing a name for your business

Choosing a name for your business isn’t something to rush into. There are several factors to consider from both a legal and marketing perspective.

Step 1: Learn how to choose a good name

Step 2: Check if a name is already taken

Step 3: Find out how to register your business name

Step 1: How to choose a good name

Keep these things in mind when choosing a name for your business:

Does it describe the product or service you offer?

Be clear about the product or service you offer. Otherwise, you may create confusion and give people the wrong impression about your business.

Does it reflect the values of your business?

Think about how you want your business to be perceived.

Can customers identify and remember it?

Names that are easy to pronounce and spell will help customers remember you. Just because a name has personal significance to you doesn’t mean that it will resonate with your customers.

Is the name distinctive?

Your business name should be unique and distinctive. For both legal and marketing reasons, you need to avoid duplication or confusion with existing business names and trademarks.

Step 2: Find out if a name is already taken

In most cases, if someone is already using a name, you cannot legally use it. By law, the name of your business can't be the same as or very similar to an existing corporate name or trade-mark. Even when it is legal to use an existing business name, it can be a disastrous marketing mistake.

There are a few places you should check to see whether a name is taken.

   Search the Internet

 

The easiest way to check if someone else is already using your proposed name is to search the Web. This will give you an idea of who else is using the same name or a similar name. You may also want to check whether the Internet domain name and social media handles you want to use are already taken. If you plan to do business in other countries, check whether anyone is using the name in those countries too.

 

href="https://www.nuans.com/auth/app/scr/corp/nuans/public/PreSearch.html?lang=eng">Search Canadian corporate names (except Quebec) and trademarks - NUANS

 

Using the NUANS database, you can search Canadian and provincial/territorial corporate names, as well as trademarks. This includes:

 

  Names of federal corporations (CD) and trademarks (TM). These both give a company exclusive rights to their use across Canada.

 

Names of provincial/territorial corporations (except Quebec).  This includes companies that are incorporated in a province or territory or extra-provincial/extra-territorial corporations that have registered in a specific province or territory. In both cases, these companies have exclusive rights to the name in that jurisdiction.

You can search your proposed name(s) for free with NUANS to find out if it is in use or not. However, you will need to pay to obtain a report to submit for name approval as a federal corporation.

Step 3: Find out how to register and protect your business name

Most businesses need to register their business name with the government. In this section, you’ll learn about the different types of names your business could have and how to register them. Before registering your business name, it is important to decide if it will be a sole proprietorship, partnership, or corporation.

Do I need to register my business name?

If you are a sole proprietor operating a business under your legal name (e.g. Jane Doe), you generally do not need to register your business name.

In all other scenarios, you will need to register your name either as your legal corporate name or a trade name. You may also wish to register a trademark.

Corporate names

If you are planning to incorporate your business, the process of incorporating includes name registration within the jurisdiction where you are incorporating.

If you incorporate federally, you will have exclusive use of your corporate name across the country. See naming your federal corporation or start the incorporation process online now.

If you are incorporating provincially or territorially, you have exclusive use of your corporate name in the province or territory where you incorporate.

Registering a trade name (operating name) other than your legal name

If you want to use a name other than your business’ legal name, you will need to register it as a trade name. Failing to register a name that you are using can result in significant fines and other legal consequences.

Some examples of businesses that need to register a trade name include:

You are a sole proprietor but are adding something to your legal name that modifies it (e.g. Jane Doe Consulting).

You plan to use a name other than your legal name for marketing purposes (e.g. your company’s legal name is Smith Bakeries Inc., but you are marketing as Bob Smith’s Bakery).

Registration of trade names is a provincial/territorial responsibility. Go to the registries of the jurisdiction(s) where you plan to do business to learn about registering your trade name.

Registering a trademark

Even if your company name or your business name is registered federally (Corporations Canada), provincially or territorially, it is recommended to also obtain trademark registration to better protect your brand. 

Advantages of registering a trademark:

The registration provides proof of ownership.

It provides you with exclusive rights across Canada for 15 years and may be renewed indefinitely.

It helps you protect your products and services from imitation and misuse.

It allows you to flag an infringement under the Trade-marks Act.

It provides you with licensing opportunities to maximize your trademark’s commercial potential.

It protects your trademark’s value.

Find out more about trademarks and learn how to register a trademark.

Glossary

Operating name and trade name

Your operating name is the name you use in your day-to-day activities and to advertise your business. For some businesses, this is the same as their legal name. Other businesses may choose to register a trade name that is different from their legal name.

Corporation

A corporation is a legal entity that is separate from its shareholders. As a shareholder of a corporation, you will not be personally liable for the debts, obligations or acts of the corporation. Businesses can incorporate federally or in a specific province/territory.

Extra-provincial or extra-territorial corporation

An extra-provincial or extra-territorial corporation is a corporation registered within one jurisdiction in Canada but doing business in another. Provincial and territorial legislation require a corporation to register as an extra-provincial or extra-territorial corporation in each province/territory where business activity occurs.

Partnership

A partnership is created when two or more individuals, or two or more corporations, do business together as partners. All partners share in the profits and the risks or debts of the business. In a general partnership, each partner is jointly liable for the debts of the partnership. In a limited partnership, a person can contribute to the business without being involved in its operations.

Sole proprietorship

With this type of business organization, you are the sole owner, and fully responsible for all debts and obligations related to your business. All profits are yours to keep.

 

Trademark

A trademark is a combination of letters, words, sounds or designs that distinguishes one company’s goods or services from those of others in the marketplace. By registering your trademark, you protect it under law from misuse by others, and you gain exclusive rights to use it throughout Canada for 15 years (a term that you can renew).
